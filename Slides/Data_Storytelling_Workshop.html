Foundation

Building a Solid Foundation

Before you open a slide deck, you need a strategic foundation. This is where you'll draft the core building blocks: your "Big Idea," "Conflict," and "Structure."

1. The Big Idea

This is the soul of your message, distilled into a single sentence. An effective "Big Idea" has 3 components (per Nancy Duarte):

1. Unique Point of View

Articulates your specific perspective.

2. What's at Stake?

Answers the 'So what?' question.

3. A Complete Sentence

A clear, declarative statement.

2. The Critical Role of Conflict

Conflict is the story's engine. It's the tension between "what is" and "what could be." Use the area below to draft your story's central conflict.

3. The Core Narrative Structure (Aristotle)

This is the framework for presenting the tension (conflict) you just identified.

ACT 1: THE BEGINNING

Establish context, present the problem (What Is).

→

ACT 2: THE MIDDLE

Present evidence, data, explore the conflict.

→

ACT 3: THE END

Deliver the solution (What Could Be) & Call to Action.

Building the 3 Acts

Turning Theory into Practice

This is the main workshop, where we build each act of the story. See how to apply structure and visualization techniques to persuade your audience.

Act 1: The Beginning – Setting the Context

To hook your audience, you must answer their 5 implicit questions (per Cliff Atkinson). Use this as your checklist:

  • 1. The Setting: When and where does the story take place?
  • 2. The Protagonist: Who is your audience?
  • 3. The Imbalance: What is the problem?
  • 4. The Desired Balance: What is the ideal future?
  • 5. The Solution: How will you bring about the change?

Act 2: The Middle – Presenting Evidence

This is where you use data to make your case. But *how* you show the data is as important as the data itself. Try the simulator below.

Act 3: The End – The Call to Action

Never finish without a clear Call to Action (CTA). Your audience has followed the story; now tell them exactly what you want them to do.

Tips for an effective CTA:

  • Be clear and specific: Don't say "Think about it." Say "Approve budget X."
  • Connect to Act 1: Restate the problem (What Is) and your solution (What Could Be).
  • Reinforce urgency: Why must this be done *now*?
Tactics

Ensuring a Clear Message

A good structure can fail if the delivery is confusing. This section provides proven tactics to make sure your audience follows along.

The Power of Repetition: Bing, Bang, Bongo

Helps an unfamiliar audience absorb and retain your core message.

Bing (Intro)

"Here's what I'm going to tell you."

Bang (Content)

"Here is the detailed content."

Bongo (Conclusion)

"Here's what I just told you."

The Importance of Structural Logic

Your presentation must be logical both horizontally and vertically.

Horizontal Logic (Titles)

Your slide titles alone should tell the story. Try this interaction:

Slide Title 5:

"2024 Budget"

"2024 Spending Projected to Exceed Budget"

Vertical Logic (Content)

Everything on a slide (chart, text) must support that one title.

  • Title: "Q3 Revenue Dropped Sharply..."
  • Chart: Must *show* the Q3 drop.
  • Color: Must *highlight* Q3.
  • Text: Must *explain* the Q3 drop.
Conclusion

The Story is for Them

The core principle to remember: the story is not for you; it is for your audience. By making the audience the protagonist, your data is no longer abstract numbers, but evidence that supports *their* journey.

Your goal is no longer to simply present data, but to tell a powerful story *with* data—a story that inspires change.
